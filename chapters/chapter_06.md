# Chapter 6

# The Department Mouse

## I. What Was Taken

The footnote came first.

This was established only in retrospect, when Dr. Tobias Wren began dating the entries in his leather notebook and realized that the footnote — clipped cleanly from a printed PDF of a 2019 article in the *Journal of Economic History*, the footnote referencing an obscure 1947 study on resource misallocation in postwar Austria — had been gone since at least the second week of term. He had assumed, when he first noticed the clean-edged absence in the margin of his printout, that he had clipped it himself. He had not. He clipped marginalia often. He would have remembered.

The letter opener was next. Professor Anneliese Drumm's letter opener, brass, engraved with her initials, a gift from her doctoral supervisor in 1998. She used it every morning on departmental correspondence. One Tuesday it was on her desk; by Wednesday it was not. She checked her bag twice, her coat pocket, the seminar table where she'd held office hours. Nothing.

Then the index cards — three of them, blank, from the stack in the seminar room that lived in a small plastic tray near the whiteboard. Janet Soo noticed this because Janet Soo noticed everything related to office supplies, having ordered those specific index cards in August and having a precise sense of how many remained.

The red pen went last. Or last as far as anyone knew. The pen had been left uncapped on a stack of dissertations awaiting grading outside Professor Orr's door. When he came out to collect them in the morning, the pen was gone. The dissertations were untouched. The cap lay nearby, placed, not dropped.

## II. The Administrator's Position

Janet Soo called Facilities on a Thursday.

"There's a mouse," she said.

"Can you describe the situation?" said the man at Facilities.

"It's stealing things."

A pause. "Stealing."

"Stealing. Specifically. Footnotes. A letter opener. Index cards. A pen." She read from her list. "An uncapped red pen. It left the cap."

Another pause, longer. "Does it seem aggressive?"

"It seems *purposeful*."

Facilities called back two days later to say they did not handle unusual wildlife situations. Janet wrote this down. She wrote down the date, the name of the man she'd spoken to, the precise phrase *unusual wildlife situations*. She put a small red dot next to the entry, her personal notation for bureaucratic failure. The page was becoming colorful.

She set three snap traps by the baseboard in the hallway. She baited them with peanut butter, which she had read was more effective than cheese. She did not read anything into the pattern of stolen items. She had read, at various points in her twenty-two years in academic administration, many patterns that turned out to be noise, and she had learned to require a higher threshold of evidence before assigning meaning.

The traps caught nothing. Each morning they were exactly where she'd left them, unsprung, the peanut butter undisturbed.

On the fourth morning, the peanut butter was gone. The traps were still unsprung.

Janet crossed out the red dot and replaced it with two.

## III. Dr. Wren's Log

*Week 3, Tuesday:* The footnote is definitely gone. Reviewing what I know about the article from which it was clipped: Brenner, "Allocative Failure as Institutional Memory," *JEH* 2019, pp. 441–489. The footnote in question was note 23, referring to Hauser (1947). I do not have access to Hauser (1947). I am not certain it was widely available. I find this interesting.

*Week 3, Thursday:* Spoke with Anneliese. The letter opener has not turned up. She is not amused but is being collegial about it, which is characteristic. I asked whether anything else was missing from her office. She said volume one of her *Order of Things* has been gone since September. I note for completeness that this is Foucault, not economics, strictly speaking, but interdisciplinary interests are not disqualifying.

*Week 4, Monday:* Three index cards. I have confirmed with Janet that these were blank — ruled, not plain. Our colleague, I think, required a specific format.

*Week 4, Wednesday:* The pen. Uncapped. I note the cap was placed, not dropped. This suggests care, deliberation, or at minimum an understanding that ink dries. I have begun to feel that our colleague is not careless with resources. They take only what they need and leave what they do not.

*Week 4, Friday:* I asked around the department quietly to see if anything else had gone missing that had not been reported. Professor Chin-Walsh mentioned that a single staple had been removed from a stapled article left on the photocopier. She thought she had unstapled it herself. I am less certain.

*Week 5, Tuesday:* I believe our colleague is composing something. I do not know what. I find I am looking forward to finding out, which is not a feeling I have had about a piece of scholarship in some years.

## IV. What Gemma Fitch Saw

Gemma Fitch was working late on a Tuesday in week two, before anyone else had noticed a pattern, before Wren had started his log, before the phrase "unusual wildlife situation" had entered Janet Soo's vocabulary. She was at the mailboxes at 9:47 PM because she had a faculty recommendation letter she needed to slip under a door and the building was empty and she didn't want to be seen doing the kind of anxious errand that announced, clearly, that she was behind on applications.

It was reading the fire safety placard.

This was what she reported afterward, and this was what no one believed, and she had been expecting them not to believe her, which is why she had not said anything for several days.

It had been sitting upright, she said. Both front paws on the placard. Moving in a way she could only describe as deliberate. Left to right. Then back. Then continuing. It had a small piece of paper near it, set flat on the floor. After a moment it picked up the paper in its teeth and carried it back the way it had come, into the gap below the stairwell door.

"It was reading," she said.

Professor Orr said, "Can you describe the paper?"

"I couldn't see it clearly. It had printing on it. Small text."

"Index-card sized?"

Gemma thought. "Maybe. Yes. Actually, maybe."

Wren wrote this down.

The following Monday, Gemma left twelve pages of her dissertation draft — chapter three, "Rational Expectations and the Persistence of Wage Stagnation" — outside her carrel door. She left them in a stack, weighted with a coffee mug. In the morning, four pages were gone. Pages seven through ten: the pages containing her main empirical argument.

She was aware this was probably not peer review. She told herself anyway that it was peer review. She had not, in four months of sending out chapter drafts, received a single comment. She had received a reading list. She had received a suggestion to "think more carefully about the framing." Four pages taken — four pages *specifically chosen* — felt, by comparison, engaged.

She left out chapter four the following week.

Six pages were taken. Gemma noted which six and began a secondary document, a record of what was taken. She titled it *Comments Received, Autumn Term*.

## V. The Argument

Professor Whitfield Orr had a theory.

He had presented it, carefully, to three colleagues. Two had left the conversation as soon as they could do so politely. One — Wren — had listened, asked a question, and then asked to see the chart.

The chart was organized as follows:

The footnote from Brenner (2019) referenced Hauser (1947), which was, Orr had determined, a study of institutional rigidity in post-conflict economic reorganization. The letter opener — brass, engraved, an artifact of professional identity and institutional authority — had belonged to the department chair. The index cards, ruled, were a tool for argument construction, for the physical arrangement of ideas into sequence. The red pen, uncapped, was for annotation, for intervention in a text, for marking what required correction.

"If you take these together," Orr said.

Wren looked at the chart for a long time.

"You're suggesting," Wren said, "that the argument is about the department."

"I am suggesting," Orr said carefully, "that the argument is *for* the department. Or *against* something about it. The tools being assembled suggest not analysis but intervention."

"A critique."

"A critique with supporting evidence." Orr tapped the footnote entry on the chart. "Beginning with primary sources."

"Our colleague," Wren said, "is building a case."

"Our colleague," said Orr, "is nearly done building it."

They looked at each other. The building was quiet. Somewhere below them, in the walls or the basement or the stairwell or wherever it was, something was working.

## VI. Professor Drumm

Professor Anneliese Drumm locked her office door for the twenty-third day in a row.

She had not slept well in three weeks. This was not, she told herself, because of the mouse — she was not the kind of person who was troubled by mice, theoretically — but because of the letter opener. The letter opener was nothing. It was brass, it had initials, it had been a gift from a man who died four years ago and whose memory she carried with more care than she carried most things, and she had owned it for twenty-six years, and it was in the walls somewhere, in the nest that nobody had found, in the service of something she did not yet understand.

Foucault without the resolution.

She had been re-reading *The Order of Things* — volume two, which was all she had — and she kept arriving at the chapter on the human sciences and feeling that something was missing, which was literally true, and then feeling that the missing thing was being used, which she had no evidence for and which was not a reasonable thing to feel about a book and a brass letter opener and a mouse.

Orr had shown her the chart. She had not told him she thought the chart was probably right.

She had told Janet to stop with the traps.

"It's not going to be caught," she said.

"It has to be caught eventually," Janet said.

"Janet," said Drumm. "When has anything in this department been caught that wasn't ready to be?"

Janet thought about this longer than either of them expected. Then she put the traps away.

## VII. The Nest (Offstage)

No one found it.

Wren suspected it was somewhere in the subbasement, near the old journal archive, in the section of shelving that had been moved twice and was no longer catalogued properly. He went down once, in the middle of a Thursday afternoon, and stood in the low-lit space between shelves of issues that had not been touched in decades, and said nothing, and listened.

He did not hear anything. But he had the strong sensation of being read.

He went back upstairs.

He added a final entry to his log: *The project is ongoing. I am not certain we will be shown the conclusion. But I note that the conclusion is not, in the end, ours to determine. Our colleague will finish when they are finished. I believe the work is serious. I believe it is close.*

He thought about adding something more. Something about what it meant to be a department that could produce — or attract, or simply fail to deter — a reader this patient, this precise, this apparently convinced that the economics of ordinary professional life contained something worth arguing about.

He didn't add it. It seemed like the kind of thing that, if you had to write it down, you had probably already missed the point.

He closed the notebook.

Below him, in the walls, something continued.
