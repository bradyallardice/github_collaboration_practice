# Chapter 6

The Wi-Fi password was `conference2025`.

It had been `conference2025` last year, when the conference was also, technically, in 2025, though a different 2025 — the fall one. The hotel's network administrator had, at some point between then and now, declined to intervene.

Priya Shah noticed first. She noticed the way she noticed everything: by entering it into a spreadsheet within four seconds of observing it. By the time she had reached the lobby elevator, she had drafted an abstract.

"It's a natural experiment," she was explaining to a man who had not yet agreed to be spoken to. "Password persistence as a signal of institutional memory decay. Or institutional memory *preservation* — we haven't decided. That's a framing question. That's actually where *you* come in."

The man, who had been trying to reach the restroom, said that he was not, strictly speaking, a methodologist.

"Neither am I," Priya said warmly. "That's why this is a team science paper."

She had, by this point, a working title (*Credential Inertia and the Epistemics of the Mid-Tier Hotel*), a preferred journal, and a shared Overleaf link that she was distributing by QR code from a laminated card she produced from a pocket that seemed to contain only laminated cards. She had identified four potential coauthors in the coffee line alone. Two of them were the same person, encountered twice, who had not realized on the second encounter that he had already declined on the first.

By the mezzanine, she had a research design. By the registration desk, she had IRB concerns. By the elevator bank, she had resolved the IRB concerns by reclassifying the study as a *commentary*.

Bethany Crisp, from her poster wedged between the coffee station and the restroom, watched Priya pass for the third time in an hour. Bethany had not been visited by anyone in six and a half hours. She had eaten all of her snacks. She had begun eating the snacks intended for the people who would visit her poster, which she had been saving as bait. She made eye contact with Priya.

This was a mistake.

"You work on institutions," Priya said, who had never seen Bethany's poster but had absorbed this fact from the air, the way certain animals locate water. "I need a third author. Fourth, actually. I'm restructuring."

"I work on eighteenth-century parish records," Bethany said.

"*Perfect,*" Priya said. "Historical baseline. You're in."

Bethany, who had not said yes, was added to the Overleaf. A push notification confirmed it.

Across the lobby, Dr. Edmund Falkworth had overheard enough to form an opinion. He formed opinions the way other people exhaled. This entire premise, he announced to no one in particular and also to three graduate students who could not escape, had been settled — *settled* — by a paper in 1987. He would not be naming the paper. Anyone serious in the field would already know it. The fact that they were pretending otherwise was, frankly, a symptom.

He did, however, ask to be cc'd on the draft.

By the time Tamsin Quill walked past with her clipboard and her walkie-talkie and the look of a woman who had just been told that the hotel's backup generator was also the hotel's primary generator, the paper had seven coauthors, a preregistration, and a Slack channel. Tamsin, who did not have time for this, paused only long enough to read the password off the lanyard of a passing attendee and murmur, to herself, with the quiet reverence of someone identifying a load-bearing wall:

"Oh. Oh, no."

She kept walking.

The paper was submitted by 4 p.m.
