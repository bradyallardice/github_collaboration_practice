# Chapter 2: The Absence

The auditorium was filling up when Tamsin realized she couldn't find Dr. Prudence Wexley-Thorne.

This was fine. Everything was fine. The keynote wasn't until 9:00 AM. It was 8:47. She still had time to locate the most celebrated—and most punctilious—scholar on the program. A woman who, according to her contract, required a specific brand of mineral water (not sparkling), a podium height of exactly 48 inches, and a green room temperature maintained at 68 degrees.

Tamsin checked her phone. Thirteen missed messages in the past hour. The hotel had lost a block of rooms. The catering company's truck was stuck on the highway. And now this.

She pressed the walkie-talkie. "Gerald, are we good on the projector for keynote?"

"Ready to go," Gerald's voice crackled back, patient as always.

At least one thing would work.

By 8:55, Tamsin had called Dr. Wexley-Thorne's hotel room five times. No answer. She'd texted her conference liaison. Nothing. She'd even tried the guest Wi-Fi to check the doctor's location tag from the app—a feature Tamsin had desperately hoped attendees would use. Dr. Wexley-Thorne hadn't checked in yet.

In the lobby, the early arrivals were beginning to gather. Marcus Halberstadt stood near the coffee station, practicing his opening: "This is more of a comment than a question..." Bethany Crisp had abandoned her poster station and was circling the crowd like a predator, eyeing name badges. Dr. Edmund Falkworth was already scoffing at something nobody had said yet. And Priya Shah was doing what she did best—extracting contact information from a junior scholar who had the misfortune of making eye contact.

Tamsin's clipboard felt heavier than it should.

At 8:59, Professor Pemberton-Crane wandered up to her, coffee in hand. "When does the keynote start?" he asked, as if he hadn't received the detailed schedule in seventeen separate emails.

"Nine o'clock," Tamsin said, her smile a fixed thing, like a painted on a doll's face.

"Is Dr. Wexley-Thorne here?"

"She will be."

She would be. She had to be.

At 9:02, Tamsin's phone buzzed. An email from the hotel manager. Dr. Wexley-Thorne had left the building at 7:15 AM. Destination unknown. She had not informed anyone.

The auditorium was nearly full now. Three hundred people, all of them expecting to be bored in exactly the way they'd come to love. Gerald was at the booth, checking levels. The podium gleamed, exactly 48 inches high. The water—the specific mineral water—was chilling in the green room, untouched.

Tamsin checked her watch: 9:04.

She could feel the questions forming. In five more minutes, the lobby would start to murmur. In ten, it would ripple into the auditorium. In fifteen, someone would ask her directly, and her practiced lie would dissolve on her tongue.

She looked around at the eight people she'd already come to know. None of them had any idea what was about to happen.

And for the first time that day, Tamsin wondered if the catering company's bankruptcy was actually the worst thing that would go wrong before lunch.
