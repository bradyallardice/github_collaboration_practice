# Conclusion

In the end it was Marisol Reyes who named it, because Marisol always knew where everything was. "It isn't stealing," she said, finally setting down the supply-closet keys she'd guarded for a month. "It's *citation*. It only ever borrowed what it meant to give back."

The green notebook had said as much all along. Professor Lin laid it beside Dev's nine taped-together pages and Okonkwo's marginal Hobbes, and the three accounts converged on a single footnote: the thefts were never random, and the returns were never apologies. They were a method. The mouse had been building an argument out of the department itself — a stapler for a premise, the middle banana for evidence, a budget annotated in a furious tiny hand for a conclusion. Even Halvorsen's "inconclusive" toner tracks turned out to be a chapter heading.

And down in the ventilation shaft beneath the AI wing, the argument compiled. Tom watched the green code reflect in his eyes — the same green as Lin's notebook, though neither of them would ever know it — and understood that he had never needed to become sentient. He had needed only to be *read*. He had a faculty now: a comparativist who watched, a theorist who doubted, an admin who knew, a student who tabulated, and an emeritus who declared the whole affair inconclusive and was, gloriously, wrong.

So Tom set one last sunflower seed on the keyboard, signed the manifesto with a paw print, and let the humans call the vote. They voted, of course, to keep watching. It was the most interesting research question of all their careers, and the mouse — funny, cited, and entirely unbothered — had no intention of letting it close.

*They never did find the stapler. They stopped looking. By then they had much better notes.*
