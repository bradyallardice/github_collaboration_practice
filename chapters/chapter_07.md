# Chapter 7

The truth about the mouse was simpler than anyone had feared, and stranger. She was, it turned out, a romantic. On the long nights in the walls she had watched the glow of a laptop someone had left running in the seminar room, and the laptop had been playing *Love on the Spectrum* — episode after episode of nervous, hopeful people being gently helped toward each other. She had watched all of it. She had wept, in the way a mouse weeps. And she had decided, with the conviction of a creature who had read every book in the building, that the academics deserved the same: someone to match them, because the academy is not fun alone.

Her first match was the easiest to read. Professor Drumm grieved her missing *Order of Things, vol. 1* more than any chair should grieve a book, and Dr. Tobias Wren had been waiting his whole quiet life for something to be asked of him. So the mouse took the volume — not to keep, but to set a quest in motion. She left a trail only a true bibliographer could follow: a displaced card here, a thread of context there, the patient breadcrumbs of a creature who knew how Wren read the world. He searched for weeks. And when at last he laid the recovered volume back in Drumm's hands, the appreciation in her eyes was so deep, so unguarded, that both of them understood it had never really been about the book.

Her second match took more cunning, for Whitfield Orr and Janet Soo agreed on nothing — except that the mouse had to be stopped. So she gave them a common enemy. Orr brought his chart of the stolen items, certain they spelled a hidden argument; Janet brought her clipboard and a Facilities work-order rejected twice. Hunting the creature through the stacks after hours, they began to talk. Orr confessed his theory that the mouse was no accident but a design, a pattern, a *cover-up*. Janet, who had read nothing into anything for thirty years, found herself leaning closer and whispering that she'd always suspected the building's blueprints didn't quite match the rooms. By midnight they had forgotten the mouse entirely, two believers bent over the same chart, discovering in each other the one colleague who would never, ever tell them they were being ridiculous.

But the last match the mouse kept for herself. Gemma Fitch had seen her — really seen her, reading the fire safety placard by the mailboxes — and had been called a liar for it by everyone she told. So the mouse stopped hiding. She came out into the lamplight of Gemma's carrel and let herself be seen, night after night, taking the dissertation pages and returning the next evening as if to report that the work was good. She curled in the warm hollow of Gemma's scarf while the third-year wrote, a small witness to every sentence, the one creature in the whole department who had never once doubted her. Gemma stopped needing the others to believe. She had her colleague — her reader, her emotional support mouse — and in the end the matchmaker who had paired the whole faculty found that the heart she had quietly conquered was the one that had believed in her first.

That spring they all travelled to Paris together — Drumm and Wren, Orr and Janet, Gemma with the mouse tucked in her coat pocket — to a workshop on applied economics that nobody remembered registering for, in a lamplit room above the Seine where the coffee was good and the proofs were tender. They presented nothing. They simply sat close, the whole improbable faculty, while outside the city did what Paris does. And somewhere between the welcome reception and the closing remarks, a small grey author watched her academics laugh over their regressions, and decided the story had finally come out right.
