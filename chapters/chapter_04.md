# Chapter 4

The note was waiting when Professor Lin unlocked her office Monday morning. Typed, single-spaced, a sunflower seed weighting one corner and a paw print inked at the bottom.

*"You have been logging me,"* it read. *"I have been logging you. Tomorrow at noon, the department votes on the budget. I will be in the room. So will everything you have lost."*

Lin's green notebook trembled in her hand. The mouse had never named a *date* before — never threatened. This was no longer a charming research question. It was a deadline.

By ten the next morning the conference room was chaos. Dev Patel had printed his spreadsheet across nine taped-together pages and pinned it to the wall like a war map. Marisol Reyes stood guarding the supply closet keys, declaring loudly that this was "still not her problem" while triple-checking the lock. Professor Okonkwo arrived skeptical as ever — until she saw, arranged neatly on the meeting table where the budget folder should have been, *everything*: her glasses, Lin's stapler, the middle banana, Halvorsen's missing toner cartridge. Every stolen item, returned. Catalogued. And in the center, the budget itself — annotated. Cited. Footnoted in a tiny, furious hand.

"It read the budget," Okonkwo whispered, for once forgetting to quote Hobbes. "It has *objections*."

Halvorsen pushed through the door with his magnifying glass, ready to declare it all inconclusive — and froze. Because there, atop the annotated pages, sitting upright and entirely unbothered, was the mouse. Seen, at last, directly.

It met their eyes, set one final seed on the table, and waited for someone to call the vote.
