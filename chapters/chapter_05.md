# Chapter 5

The panel had been titled "The Future of the Discipline," which in retrospect was optimistic.

Eighteen minutes in, Dr. Edmund Falkworth had set down his prepared remarks, produced a manuscript from his briefcase — spiral-bound, 214 pages, the cover sheet read *DRAFT: DO NOT CITE (please cite)* — and simply begun reading. Not summarizing. Reading. From page one.

The moderator, a junior faculty member from a Big Ten school who had agreed to this role before fully understanding what it entailed, had attempted two interruptions. Both had failed. She now sat with her chin resting on her hand, her name placard slightly askew, displaying the specific expression of someone recalibrating their career timeline.

In the audience, something unexpected was happening: people were taking notes. Not polite, performative notes — real ones, frantic ones, the kind usually reserved for job talks. A postdoc in the third row had filled both sides of a legal pad and was now using the back of a conference program. Two graduate students near the aisle were typing without looking at their keyboards.

Nobody could have said exactly why. The manuscript was not obviously good. Falkworth had already used the phrase "as I have long argued" eleven times, each citation pointing back to himself. But there was something about the sheer audacity of it — the unbroken eye contact with no one in particular, the total indifference to the session's supposed topic — that had tipped over from insufferable into somehow compelling.

He turned to page forty-seven. The room was very quiet. Someone's phone buzzed and they silenced it without looking down.

"Methodology," Falkworth said, "is a matter of conviction." He paused for effect. "This was established in 1987."

He did not name the paper. He never named the paper. The postdoc wrote it down anyway.
