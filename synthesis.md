# The Department Mouse: A Story

It began with a footnote.

No one noticed at first. Dr. Tobias Wren, the department's unofficial bibliographer, simply assumed he had clipped it himself — a note from a 2019 article referencing an obscure 1947 study on resource misallocation in postwar Austria. He clipped marginalia often. But he hadn't clipped this one.

What followed was methodical. Professor Anneliese Drumm's brass letter opener — a gift from her doctoral supervisor, used every morning for twenty-six years — vanished on a Wednesday. Three ruled index cards disappeared from the seminar room. Then an uncapped red pen, taken cleanly from a stack of dissertations, its cap placed beside them, not dropped. Janet Soo, the department administrator, called Facilities. Facilities said they did not handle unusual wildlife situations. The traps she set went unsprung for three mornings; on the fourth, the peanut butter was gone and not a single trap had fired.

Meanwhile, Gemma Fitch — third-year PhD student, the one nobody believed — had already seen it. Working late by the mailboxes at 9:47 PM, she watched a mouse sitting upright at the fire safety placard, reading. Left to right, then back. Deliberate. It had a small printed page beside it. It picked the page up in its teeth and disappeared under the stairwell door. When she reported this, no one believed her.

But she left out pages of her dissertation anyway. Twelve pages of chapter three, weighted with a coffee mug. By morning, four were gone — precisely pages seven through ten, her main empirical argument. She titled her record of the thefts *Comments Received, Autumn Term*. The following week she left out chapter four. Six more pages were taken. In the margin of the last page before the gap, she found a small circle, precisely drawn, around a single sentence: *The archive cannot account for what it did not think to keep.* She opened a new document and began to write.

Professor Whitfield Orr made a chart. The footnote pointed to a study of institutional rigidity. The letter opener was an artifact of professional authority. The index cards were tools for sequencing an argument. The red pen was for annotation, for intervention. He showed the chart to Wren, who looked at it for a long time.

"You're suggesting," Wren said, "that the argument is about the department."

"I'm suggesting," Orr said carefully, "that it is *for* the department."

Drumm, who had been re-reading volume two of *The Order of Things* — all she had left — told Janet to stop with the traps. "When has anything in this department been caught," she said, "that wasn't ready to be?" Janet thought about this longer than either of them expected, then put the traps away.

The truth, when it became clear, was stranger than a critique. The mouse was a romantic. She had watched *Love on the Spectrum* on a forgotten laptop in the seminar room, episode after episode, and had decided that the academics deserved the same: someone to match them. She took Drumm's Foucault not to keep it but to set a quest in motion — a trail only a bibliographer could follow — so that Wren would return it, and in Drumm's unguarded gratitude, both would understand it had never been about the book. She gave Orr and Janet a common enemy, sent them hunting through the stacks together after hours, and by midnight they had forgotten the mouse entirely, bent over the same chart, each discovering in the other the one colleague who would never call them ridiculous.

And for Gemma, who had seen her and been called a liar for it, the mouse simply stopped hiding. She came into the lamplight of the carrel and stayed — taking pages, returning the next night, curling in the hollow of Gemma's scarf while she wrote. The one creature in the building who had never once doubted her.

That spring they all went to Paris. Drumm and Wren. Orr and Janet. Gemma with the mouse in her coat pocket. A workshop on applied economics that nobody remembered registering for, in a lamplit room above the Seine. They presented nothing. They simply sat close, the whole improbable faculty, while outside the city did what Paris does. And somewhere between the welcome reception and the closing remarks, a small grey author watched her academics laugh over their regressions, and decided the story had finally come out right.
