# The Conference from Hell

## A Short Story

### I. Morning

At 9:04 AM, Tamsin Quill stood in the mouth of the auditorium and understood, with the cold clarity that only sleep deprivation can provide, that Dr. Prudence Wexley-Thorne was not coming back.

The hotel manager's email had been blunt. Checked out at 7:15 AM. Left no forwarding. The specific mineral water — not sparkling — sweated quietly in the green room, a monument to a keynote that would not happen. The podium, calibrated to exactly 48 inches, waited for a woman who had, sometime between breakfast and the conference program, decided she was done.

Tamsin keyed her walkie-talkie.

"Gerald."

"Here."

"I need a situation."

A pause. Gerald was famously economical with words, and the pause was, for him, a kind of speech. "Projector's up. Mic's hot. Clicker charged. What's wrong?"

"The keynote."

"Gone?"

"Gone."

Another pause. "I can loop the sizzle reel."

"For ninety minutes?"

"It's a long sizzle reel."

It was not, in fact, a long sizzle reel. Tamsin had edited it herself at 2 AM. It was four minutes and seventeen seconds, and the last eleven seconds were a slow zoom on the university's new logo, which nobody liked.

She scanned the auditorium. Marcus Halberstadt was in the front row, already holding a pen aloft as though the Q&A were imminent and perpetual. Priya Shah was in the aisle, collecting names with the brisk efficiency of a census worker in an emergency. Professor Pemberton-Crane was in seat 3C, reading a book that was not on the panel.

And Edmund Falkworth — Dr. Edmund Falkworth, who had a panel at 2 PM and was not scheduled to speak one minute before — was in the fifth row, scoffing at the program in his lap.

Tamsin watched him scoff. She watched him underline something. She watched him scoff again, louder, a performative scoff, a scoff looking for an audience.

She had, she realized, exactly one option.

---

"Dr. Falkworth."

He looked up. His expression was that of a man who had been waiting, for some decades, to be approached in exactly this way.

"Dr. Wexley-Thorne is indisposed."

"She's left, hasn't she."

"She's indisposed."

Falkworth nodded slowly, as though this confirmed something he had long argued. He glanced at his briefcase — the spiral-bound manuscript was already inside, as it always was, as it had been for eleven years. He closed his program with the soft, satisfied snap of a man accepting a prize he had, in some sense, earned.

### II. The Reading

Gerald dimmed the lights at 9:11. The sizzle reel played. It was, as expected, four minutes and seventeen seconds long, and nobody enjoyed the logo.

At 9:16, Dr. Edmund Falkworth took the stage.

He did not use the mineral water. He did not adjust the podium, though he was shorter than Dr. Wexley-Thorne by several inches and had to tilt his chin up to reach the microphone. He produced the manuscript — *DRAFT: DO NOT CITE (please cite)* — and set it on the lectern with the reverence of a priest placing a relic.

"Methodology," he began, "is a matter of conviction."

He paused.

"This was established in 1987."

He did not name the paper. He turned to page one.

What happened next, nobody could later explain. The postdoc in row three opened a fresh legal pad. Marcus Halberstadt, who had opened his mouth to interject at minute four, closed it again at minute five and never reopened it for the duration. Pemberton-Crane set down his book. Priya Shah paused mid-pitch and sat, actually sat, in a chair, something she had not done in three days.

Even Gerald, in the booth, watching the levels, found himself leaning forward.

Falkworth read. He read for ninety minutes without pause or apology. He read through eleven self-citations. He read past the scheduled coffee break. There was something about the sheer audacity of it — the unbroken eye contact with no one in particular, the total indifference to the session's supposed topic — that had tipped over from insufferable into somehow compelling.

At 10:46 AM, he turned the final page, closed the manuscript, and looked up.

"Questions?" he said.

Marcus Halberstadt's hand shot up with the speed of a man who had been waiting his entire life.

"This is more of a comment than a question," he began.

The room laughed. The laughter of a room that has survived something together.

### III. The Lobby

The conference did not recover its equilibrium after that. It simply found a new one.

Priya Shah, having been briefly sedentary, emerged from the auditorium with renewed purpose and, within minutes, a theory. The Wi-Fi password, she had discovered, was `conference2025`. It had been `conference2025` last year — a different 2025, the fall one. This was, she announced to a man trying to reach the restroom, a natural experiment.

By the mezzanine, she had a research design. By the registration desk, she had IRB concerns. By the elevator bank, she had resolved the IRB concerns by reclassifying the study as a *commentary*. She had identified four potential coauthors in the coffee line alone. Two of them were the same person, encountered twice, who had not realized on the second encounter that he had already declined on the first.

Across the lobby, she swept past the poster session. This was where Bethany Crisp lived now.

Bethany's poster — *Marginal Voices at the Margins: A Marginal Analysis* — hung perfectly level between the coffee station and the door to the men's restroom. She had used a laser level. She had laminated the abstract. She had printed a QR code that linked to her pre-print, her email, and a Calendly link with 47 open slots.

No one had stopped. Not once.

The granola bars had been gone by noon. The trail mix had followed around two. The emergency chocolate Bethany kept in her laptop bag for *real emergencies* had not survived the three o'clock panel on post-structural frameworks in contemporary blah blah blah.

Forty-three people had refilled their coffee. Nineteen of them had made eye contact with her poster. Fourteen had looked away before she could speak. Four had looked directly at her and still looked away. One man had said "nice work" while clearly reading a different poster entirely.

Priya made eye contact with her.

This was a mistake — for Priya.

"You work on institutions," Priya said, who had never seen Bethany's poster but had absorbed this fact from the air, the way certain animals locate water. "I need a fourth author. I'm restructuring."

"I work on eighteenth-century parish records," Bethany said, with the blank courtesy of a person who has not eaten in six hours.

"*Perfect,*" Priya said. "Historical baseline. You're in."

Bethany, who had not said yes, was added to the Overleaf. A push notification confirmed it.

Dr. Edmund Falkworth, passing through the lobby to accept congratulations nobody had offered, overheard enough to form an opinion. This entire premise, he announced to no one in particular and also to three graduate students who could not escape, had been settled — *settled* — by a paper in 1987. He would not be naming the paper. He did, however, ask to be cc'd on the draft.

The paper was submitted by 4 PM.

### IV. The Return

At 4:47 PM, a conference attendee drifted too close to Bethany's poster.

Bethany straightened. Her lanyard swayed. Something shifted behind her eyes.

"Hi," she said. "Can I tell you about my methodology?"

The attendee had only wanted more coffee.

They did not get more coffee.

---

Meanwhile, in the main auditorium, Dr. Prudence Wexley-Thorne had returned.

Nobody could say when exactly she'd come back. She had posted a cryptic message to her academic blog about "rediscovering the pastoral" and was, apparently, no longer in Vermont. She had simply appeared at the podium — the correct podium, at the correct 48 inches — and begun to deliver an unscheduled, impromptu lecture, a thing she had not cleared with Tamsin and would not be clearing with Tamsin, because Tamsin's walkie-talkie had gone silent three hours ago and no one dared ask whether the batteries had died or whether it had been thrown across the green room.

Three people sat in the auditorium. They were too polite to leave.

Gerald the AV Guy sat in the back row with a coffee, watching the projector display the word BUFFERING for the fourteenth time. He had clocked out emotionally around noon. The mic was hot. The clicker was charged. None of it, at this point, was required.

Marcus Halberstadt raised his hand.

"This is more of an observation than a question," he began, settling in, "but..."

Tamsin's eye twitched. She very deliberately did not reach for the fire alarm.

At the hotel bar, Bethany Crisp was telling a man in a blazer about her methodology. The man was nodding politely, slowly backing away, toward the door, toward the parking lot, toward anywhere that was not here.

He would not make it.

Nobody was going to make it.

The conference had twenty-one more hours to run.
